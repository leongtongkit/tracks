A free Ableton Live alternative that runs in any browser

By Tongkit Leong · July 23, 2026 · 7 min read

Ableton Live is a paid, install-only DAW sold in tiers, best known for its session view and clip-launching workflow. Tracks is a free browser DAW with its own session view for launching loop clips, plus a piano roll, built-in synths, and a full mixer, and it opens in a tab with no purchase, no install, and no account.

The core difference: install vs. tab

Ableton Live is desktop software for Windows and Mac. You download and install it, then unlock features according to which edition you bought — Intro is the cheapest and most limited, Standard sits in the middle, and Suite adds the full instrument and Max for Live device library at the highest price. Every tier is a real purchase, and moving from a starter edition to a more capable one later usually means paying the difference.

Tracks skips all of that. There is no installer, no edition to pick, and no upgrade path. You open tracks.jfound.net in Chrome, Firefox, Edge, or Safari — on Windows, Mac, Linux, or a Chromebook — and every feature is available immediately, for free, with nothing gated behind a purchase.

Where Ableton Live is still the stronger tool

Ableton Live's biggest advantage over any browser DAW, including Tracks, is VST/AU plugin support — you can load third-party synths, effects, and mastering plugins from the wider commercial and free plugin market and run them inside Live's Session and Arrangement views. Tracks does not support external plugins; every instrument and effect is built into the engine itself. If a specific third-party synth or a Max for Live device is central to your sound, Live is the only option that supports it.

Live's audio warping is also a category-defining feature — it can stretch and repitch audio in real time with several selectable algorithms tuned for different material (beats, vocals, complex mixes), which goes deeper than most browser tools attempt. And because a Live license is a one-time purchase for that edition (with paid upgrades between major versions), producers who buy in tend to stick with it for years without a recurring subscription.

Where Tracks is the better starting point

Cost and access

Tracks costs nothing to try or to keep using. There is no Intro/Standard/Suite decision to make and no feature locked behind a higher tier. If you're deciding whether music production software is worth paying for at all, or you just want to finish one track today, that removes the decision entirely.

Session view, without the purchase

Live popularized the session view — a grid of loop clips you launch and combine live instead of arranging on a fixed timeline — and Tracks has its own version of that workflow built in for free. See what is the session view? Loop-based songwriting in a browser DAW for how it works and how to turn a jam into a finished arrangement on the timeline.

Instruments built in, no browsing required

Tracks ships a subtractive synth and an FM synth for building your own patches from oscillators and filters, 808/909-style drum machines, a 16-pad sampler, and SoundFont (.sf2) playback — see the best free VST alternative instruments in the browser for a full rundown. None of it requires downloading a separate plugin or instrument pack; it's available the moment the tab loads, unlike Live's Instrument and Sound packs, several of which are only bundled into the pricier editions.

Mixing depth without plugin shopping

Every track in Tracks has a parametric EQ, compressor, gate, and de-esser built in, plus reverb and delay sends, group buses, sidechain ducking, and automation on any parameter — see how to mix a track: EQ, compression, and reverb basics. In Live, a comparably deep chain on every track usually means adding stock or third-party devices per channel. In Tracks, it's already there.

Recording, vocals, and stems

Tracks records mic input with count-in and take comping, and a MIDI keyboard over Web MIDI with no drivers. It includes built-in autotune, time-stretch/repitch, and stem extraction that splits an imported audio file into vocals, drums, bass, and other. Live can do all of this too, generally well, but usually through a mix of stock devices and Max for Live rather than a single feature that's on by default in every edition.

Privacy and local storage

Tracks never uploads a project anywhere — everything, including recorded audio, stays on your device in local browser storage. Live is a local desktop application too, so on that point they're philosophically similar: neither one requires cloud storage of your work by default. See where your project actually lives for how Tracks' local-only storage works and how to back up a project file.

Which one should you use?

Choose Ableton Live if

You already own a license, you rely on specific third-party VST plugins or Max for Live devices, or you need Live's deeper audio-warping algorithms for heavily time-stretched material. It's also the better fit if you plan to produce professionally for years on a fixed desktop setup and want a one-time purchase per edition rather than depending on a browser tab.

Choose Tracks if

You want session-view, clip-launch songwriting today without buying an edition, installing anything, or creating an account, and you don't need third-party plugin hosting. Tracks covers the instruments, mixing, recording, and export workflow most producers actually use, and because it's local-only, your audio never leaves your device either way.

Frequently asked questions

Is Ableton Live free to try?

Ableton offers a time-limited trial of Live, after which you need to buy a license to keep using it. Tracks has no trial period or expiration — it's free to use for as long as you want, every time you open the tab.

Can Tracks load VST plugins like Ableton Live can?

No. Browsers don't support hosting native VST/AU plugins, so Tracks — like any browser DAW — ships its instruments and effects built into the engine instead. If a specific third-party plugin or Max for Live device is central to your sound, Live is the only path to using it.

Does Tracks have a session view like Ableton Live's?

Yes. Tracks includes a clip-launch session view for building loops live and combining them, the same core idea Live popularized. See what is the session view? for how it works and how to turn launched clips into an arranged song.

Can I move a project between Ableton Live and Tracks?

Not directly — Live project files (.als) and Tracks project files (.tracks.json) are different formats, and neither reads the other's native file. You can bridge between them with standard interchange formats: export a standard MIDI file from Tracks (see how to export a MIDI file) to bring notes into Live, or export stems or a WAV/MP3 mixdown to move finished audio the other way.

Is Tracks good enough to finish a full track without Ableton Live?

Yes. Tracks covers arrangement, session-view songwriting, instruments, recording, mixing, mastering-level loudness tools, and WAV/MP3/stem/MIDI export — the full path from a blank project to a finished, exported song, as covered in how to make a song in your browser for beginners. Whether it's "enough" depends only on whether you need the third-party plugins or Max for Live devices that Live can host and Tracks can't.
