How to write a bassline that locks with the kick drum

By Tongkit Leong · July 15, 2026 · 7 min read

A bassline locks with the kick when the two share rhythmic space instead of landing on top of each other — write your kick pattern first, then place bass notes in the gaps around it, and use a narrow low-end EQ cut to keep the two from masking each other. You can do all of this in the free Tracks browser studio with no plugins.

Why kick and bass fight each other in the first place

The kick drum and the bassline are almost always the two loudest things happening in the same frequency range at the same time. A kick's punch usually lives somewhere between 50–100 Hz, with a click around 2–4 kHz, and a bass note's fundamental often sits in that same 40–120 Hz zone. When a kick hit and a bass note land at the exact same instant, both are asking for the same low-end headroom, and the result is a mix that sounds thick and undefined instead of punchy.

This is not primarily an EQ problem, even though EQ can help clean it up afterward. It is a rhythm problem. If the kick and bass are written to never actually compete for the same instant, most of the mud disappears before you touch a single fader. That is what "locking" means: the bass is arranged around the kick's rhythm, not simply present at the same time as it.

Step 1: Program the kick first

Build your kick pattern in the Tracks drum machine before you write a single bass note. The kick is the fixed reference point — everything else, including the bass, gets arranged around it. A simple four-on-the-floor pattern, a syncopated hip-hop kick, or a sparse trap pattern with rolls all give the bass a different rhythmic shape to lock into, so decide on the kick's feel before you touch the piano roll.

Loop just the kick pattern on its own for a few bars and internalize where the gaps are — the beats and half-beats where no kick is playing. Those gaps are where the bassline has room to move.

Step 2: Place bass notes around the kick, not on top of it

Open the piano roll on your bass track and place notes so they start on or just after a kick hit, rather than stacking a new bass note directly under every kick. A common, reliable pattern: let the kick hit alone on beat one, then bring the bass note in a sixteenth note later, or hold the bass note from the previous beat through the kick instead of retriggering it.

This does two things. It gives the kick a clean instant to be heard without competing pitch content, and it gives the bassline its own rhythmic identity instead of just doubling the kick pattern an octave up. Listen back with just kick and bass soloed — you should be able to hear the kick's transient clearly even when the bass is present.

A useful reference point: think of the kick and bass as two people trading short phrases rather than two people talking over each other. The kick speaks, the bass answers in the space left behind.

Step 3: Shorten notes that overlap the next kick

Long, sustained bass notes are the most common cause of a bassline that clashes with the kick, because a note that is still ringing when the next kick lands forces the two to overlap regardless of where the note started. In the piano roll, look at any bass note that stretches close to or past the next kick hit and drag its right edge shorter, leaving a small rest immediately before that kick.

That short rest — even just a thirty-second note of silence — is often all it takes to give the kick a clear window to punch through. You are not making the bassline sparse; you are giving the low end room to breathe on every downbeat that matters.

Step 4: Keep the bassline in one register

Write the bassline in a narrow, consistent octave range rather than jumping around unpredictably. A bass that stays within roughly one octave keeps the low end predictable, which makes it much easier to carve space for the kick with EQ later, since you know approximately where the bass's energy sits at all times.

Jumping the bassline up an octave for a fill or a transition is fine and often useful — just treat it as a deliberate accent, not the default behavior of the part.

Step 5: Carve space with EQ where the two still overlap

Even a well-arranged kick and bass will overlap sometimes, and that is fine — a small parametric EQ move on each track handles the rest. On the bass track, in the Tracks mixer, add a gentle cut around the kick's dominant low frequency (often somewhere in the 60–90 Hz range, but use your ears and the EQ's frequency display, not a fixed number). On the kick track, you can do the inverse: a small dip around the bass's fundamental frequency so the kick's body doesn't smear into the bass's territory.

Keep these cuts subtle — a decibel or two is usually enough. The goal is to give each instrument its own small pocket of frequency space, not to hollow either one out. If you find yourself cutting more than 3–4 dB to make room, the rhythmic overlap from steps 2–3 is probably still the bigger issue, and no amount of EQ will fully fix a timing collision. See How to mix a track: EQ, compression, and reverb basics for the fundamentals of using the EQ panel.

If you want the kick to duck the bass automatically every time it hits, instead of relying on EQ alone, Tracks also has built-in sidechain ducking on the mixer — see Sidechain compression explained for how to set that up. Sidechaining and rhythmic locking solve overlapping problems in different ways and often work well combined: locking handles the arrangement, sidechain ducking handles the moments where the two still land together on purpose.

Step 6: Check the low end in mono

Solo the kick and bass together and switch your monitoring — or just listen carefully — to check how the pair sounds summed. Low frequencies are where phase problems and masking are most audible, and a bassline that sounded fine in isolation can suddenly feel hollow or weak once it is combined with the kick. If the kick seems to disappear under the bass, or the bass loses definition under the kick, go back to steps 2 and 3 before reaching for more EQ — the rhythmic pocket usually needs another pass.

Once kick and bass feel locked together — punchy, defined, and neither one burying the other — bring in the rest of the arrangement. A bassline that works against a solo kick will keep working once the rest of the track is layered on top, because the foundation is solid.

Frequently asked questions

What does it mean for a bassline to "lock" with the kick drum?

It means the bassline's rhythm is arranged around the kick pattern's gaps rather than simply playing at the same time as it. Instead of a bass note landing on every kick hit and competing for the same low-end space, the bass note starts just after the kick, holds through it, or rests just before the next kick lands — so the two trade rhythmic space instead of colliding in it.

Should I write the kick or the bassline first?

Write the kick pattern first. It is the simpler, more fixed rhythmic element, and it gives you a clear reference grid to write the bassline against. Writing the bassline first and fitting a kick around it later usually produces a muddier result, because the bass ends up with no consistent gaps for the kick to occupy.

Do I need sidechain compression to make kick and bass work together?

No — rhythmic arrangement (leaving gaps, shortening overlapping notes) solves most of the problem on its own, and it is the foundation you should build first. Sidechain ducking is a useful additional tool for moments where the kick and bass deliberately land together, especially in dance and electronic genres, but it is not a substitute for a well-arranged bassline. See Sidechain compression explained for how it works.

What frequency range should I EQ to fix kick and bass clashes?

There is no single fixed number — it depends on your specific kick sample and bass sound. Use the EQ panel's frequency display in the Tracks mixer to sweep and listen for where each element's energy is concentrated, usually somewhere between 40–100 Hz for both. A small cut of a decibel or two on one element around the frequency where the other one is strongest is usually enough; large cuts are a sign the underlying arrangement needs another pass first.

Why does my bass sound fine alone but muddy with the kick?

This is the classic masking problem — two sounds with overlapping frequency content and overlapping timing become harder to distinguish when played together, even if each sounds clean in isolation. Solo the kick and bass together (not just the bass alone) while you write and mix, since that is the actual context the bassline has to work in.
