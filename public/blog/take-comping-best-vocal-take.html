How to comp the best take from multiple recordings

By Tongkit Leong · July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

Take comping means recording the same phrase several times and then assembling one final performance out of the best moments from each pass. In the Tracks studio, every recording stacks as a take on the same clip, so you can arm the track, record until you have a handful of passes, then pick and splice the strongest sections together — free, with nothing to install.

Why comp instead of chasing one perfect take

Almost no one nails a vocal line or a guitar solo in a single pass all the way through. The verse might be perfect on take two, but the chorus only really lands on take five. Comping accepts that and turns it into a workflow: record enough raw material, then edit for the best result instead of performing for it. It's how most professional vocal and solo tracks actually get made — the "one take wonder" you hear on a finished record is usually built from several.

This matters just as much for instruments as it does for vocals. A guitar solo, a bassline played live, or a synth lead recorded in from a MIDI keyboard can all be comped the same way — the take lane doesn't care what's making the sound. See how to use a MIDI keyboard in a browser DAW if you're recording an instrument part this way for the first time.

1. Arm the track and turn on a count-in

Add an audio track for a mic or line source, or a MIDI/instrument track if you're playing a part live from a MIDI keyboard. Arm the track, then turn on the count-in (one or two bars works well) so every pass starts from the same downbeat instead of a cold start. If the section is short, loop it — Tracks will keep recording a fresh take on every pass through the loop instead of overwriting the last one.

2. Record more passes than you think you need

Press record and play or sing the part through, mistakes and all. Don't stop to fix a flub — that's what comping is for. Do it again. Then again. Four to six passes is usually enough to have real options; fewer than that and you'll end up stretching a mediocre phrase because nothing better exists. Each pass is captured as its own take without touching the ones before it, so nothing you already recorded is at risk.

3. Open the take lane and listen critically

Expand the clip to reveal its stacked takes. Solo the track and audition each one, section by section rather than start to finish — you're not looking for the single best full take, you're looking for the best two or four bars inside each one. Note where the timing is tightest, the tone is cleanest, and the performance has the most energy. It helps to do this listening pass with fresh ears; if you've been staring at the same section for twenty minutes, take a break first.

4. Build the composite take

Once you know which take has the best version of each phrase, select those sections and comp them into one continuous performance on the clip. Work phrase by phrase — a natural breath, a rest between lines, or a bar boundary is a much better edit point than the middle of a held note or a fast run. The goal is a single take that sounds like it was performed in one pass, even though it was assembled from several.

Tips for a clean comp

  • Match levels first. If one take was sung or played noticeably louder or softer than the others, even out the gain before you judge which phrase sounds "better" — a louder take can trick your ear into preferring it.
  • Cut on silence or a soft consonant. Splicing during a sustained note or a loud transient is where edits get audible. A gap in the breath, or the start of a word like "t" or "k," hides a cut well.
  • Let the crossfade do its job. A short crossfade at each splice blends the waveform edges together so the join doesn't click or pop. If an edit still sounds abrupt, nudge the fade a little wider.
  • Don't over-comp. Stitching together too many tiny fragments from too many takes can make a performance sound stiff and inconsistent in tone, even if every individual phrase was technically the best available. A comp built from three or four longer sections usually beats one built from fifteen short ones.

5. Finish the performance

Once the comp is solid, treat it like any other recorded take. If it's a vocal, set your project's key in the transport bar and apply the built-in autotune to smooth out pitch — see how to record and tune vocals in your browser for the full workflow. Add EQ to shape the tone, a compressor to even out dynamics, and a send to the reverb bus for space. If it's an instrument, balance it in the mix the same way you would a single unedited take — the comp is now just a track.

6. Export

When the song is finished, export it to WAV or MP3, or bounce individual stems if you're finishing the mix somewhere else. Every take you recorded, and the comp you built from them, stays local to your device the whole time — nothing is uploaded during recording or editing.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need special software to comp takes?

No. Take comping is built into the free Tracks browser studio — record multiple passes on the same clip and the take lane keeps them all available to edit from.

Can I comp instrument parts, not just vocals?

Yes. Anything recorded through a mic, a line input, or a MIDI keyboard stacks takes the same way, so guitar solos, bass parts, and synth leads can all be comped.

Will comping ruin the original takes?

No. Comping in Tracks is non-destructive — every take you recorded stays intact underneath the comp, so you can always go back and choose a different section later.

How many takes should I record before I start comping?

Four to six good-faith passes is a solid starting point for most parts. Fewer and you may not have real alternatives to choose from; a lot more and the listening pass starts taking longer than the performance did.
