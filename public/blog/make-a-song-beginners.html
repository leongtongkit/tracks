How to make a song in your browser for beginners

By Tongkit Leong · June 14, 2026 · 7 min read

To make a song in your browser as a beginner: open the Tracks studio, set a tempo and key, build a drum beat, draw a chord progression on a synth track, add a bassline and melody, arrange them into song sections, and export to WAV or MP3. You need nothing installed — the whole process runs in the page, free, and your project never leaves your device.

What you need

A modern browser (Chrome, Edge, Firefox, or Safari) and a device with a working internet connection the first time you load the page. After that, the studio can run offline. You do not need an account, a credit card, or any software download. A pair of headphones or speakers helps a lot.

If you have a MIDI keyboard, you can plug it in via USB — Tracks picks it up automatically through the browser's Web MIDI API. If not, your computer keyboard and mouse are enough to write every part.

Step 1: Open the studio and set your tempo and key

Go to the Tracks studio and click anywhere to start the audio engine (browsers require a user gesture before playing sound). You will see the transport bar at the top. Set two things before you write a note:

  • BPM (tempo). Start simple: pop and bedroom-pop around 90–120 BPM; hip-hop 80–100; electronic and dance 120–130. You can change it later — Tracks even supports a tempo map if you want the song to speed up or slow down.
  • Project key. Choose a key like C major or A minor. Setting the key tells Tracks which scale to highlight in the piano roll, so it is much easier to play in tune without knowing theory.

Step 2: Build a drum beat

Click the "+" button to add your first track, and choose a drum track. You will get a choice of an 808 or 909 drum machine kit. Open the drum machine panel and you will see rows labeled Kick, Snare, Hi-Hat (Closed), Hi-Hat (Open), and several percussion voices.

The classic beginner pattern that works for almost any genre:

  • Kick: beat 1 and beat 3.
  • Snare: beat 2 and beat 4.
  • Closed hi-hat: the off-beat ("and") between each beat, giving you eight evenly spaced hits per bar.

Tap the pads to audition the sounds, then draw the pattern on the grid or in the piano roll. The labeled rows make it clear which pitch triggers which drum. Once the loop sounds right, click the clip in the timeline and drag it right to repeat it for as many bars as you need.

Want to go deeper on beats? See the step-by-step beat-making guide.

Step 3: Write a chord progression

Add a second track and choose a synthesizer instrument. Pick a pad, piano, or keys preset — something that sustains a note so you can hear the chords clearly.

In music, a chord is three or more notes played at the same time. A chord progression is a sequence of chords that repeats under your song. You do not need music theory to use one. Here are three four-chord progressions that work in almost any key:

  • I – V – vi – IV (C – G – Am – F in C major): the most common pop progression in history.
  • I – IV – V – I (C – F – G – C): classic and resolved.
  • vi – IV – I – V (Am – F – C – G): a minor-key feel with emotional pull.

In the piano roll, draw a chord by stacking three notes vertically — the root, the note four semitones up, and the note seven semitones up (a major triad). The highlighted scale notes in the piano roll help you land in key. Make each chord one or two bars long, then loop the four-bar block.

Step 4: Add a bassline

The easiest way to get a bassline that fits your chords: duplicate the chord track, select all the notes, and delete everything except the lowest note of each chord. Then use the piano roll's transpose control to drop those notes down one or two octaves. Pick a bass preset (sine bass, 808 sub, or electric bass) for the track. The result is a bassline that locks perfectly to your chords because it is playing the root of each one.

To add some rhythm, shorten the held notes and add a short repeated note on each beat — that "boom-boom-boom" low-end pulse works across nearly every genre.

Step 5: Write a melody

Add another synth track for the lead melody. A melody is a single-note line that sits on top of the chords. Here is the simplest approach that always works: stick to the notes highlighted in the piano roll (the scale notes) and start most phrases on a note that belongs to the current chord.

Draw three to six notes for a short phrase, leave a gap, then answer it with another phrase. Repetition with small variation is the core of every memorable melody. If you have a MIDI keyboard, record a take in real time — press the record button, and Tracks will capture your playing with count-in.

Step 6: Record vocals (optional)

If you want to sing or rap over the beat, add an audio track and click the microphone icon. Tracks will ask permission to use your mic — allow it, then hit record. You hear the backing track through your headphones while recording. After the take, you can comp the best sections, trim the clip, and run the built-in autotune to correct pitch. For a detailed walkthrough, see the guide to recording vocals in the browser.

Step 7: Arrange the song

So far you have a loop. To build a song, you need structure: typically intro, verse, chorus, bridge, and outro. In the Tracks timeline:

  • Right-click clips to copy them.
  • Paste them into new positions to build song sections.
  • Mute certain tracks in the verse (for example, drop the melody out) to create contrast with the chorus, where everything comes in together.
  • Use markers to label sections so you can find your place quickly.

A three-minute song at 100 BPM has about 75 bars. A simple structure: 8-bar intro, 16-bar verse, 8-bar chorus, 16-bar verse, 8-bar chorus, 8-bar outro. Adjust to taste.

Step 8: Mix and export

Open the mixer panel. Each track has a volume fader and a pan knob. The goal is for the kick and snare to punch through, the bass to fill the low end without muddying it, and the melody or vocals to sit clearly on top. A few practical tips:

  • Start with everything at about 70% volume so you have headroom to boost what matters.
  • Add a small reverb send on the melody and vocals to give them space — but keep the kick, snare, and bass dry.
  • Use the per-track parametric EQ to cut any mud in the 200–400 Hz range on the bass if it feels boxy.

When the mix sounds balanced on your headphones, go to File and choose Export. WAV gives you the highest quality — use it if you plan to master the track later. MP3 gives a smaller file that is easier to share. Both are free and unlimited.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to know music theory to make a song?

No. The scale highlighting in the piano roll shows you which notes fit together. The chord progressions above are ready to use without understanding why they work. Theory helps over time, but a beginner can finish a full song in a single session without it.

Can I save my project and come back to it later?

Yes. Tracks saves your project locally in your browser. You can also export a .tracks.json project file from the File menu and reload it later — useful if you work on multiple devices or want a backup on disk.

How long does it take to make a song?

A first, rough song can come together in an hour. Most people spend several sessions refining a track — adding detail to the arrangement, improving the mix, and rewriting parts that feel weak. The browser studio is always open, so you can return to a project anytime.

Can I use my own audio samples?

Yes. Drag an audio file onto the Tracks timeline to import it as a clip, or load samples into the 16-pad sampler. You can also load a SoundFont (.sf2) file if you have a specific instrument you want to use.

What if my project gets too large to run well?

Browser studios run on your CPU, so very large projects with many tracks can slow down on older hardware. If you notice glitches, reduce the number of simultaneous effects, freeze heavy tracks (bounce them to audio), or export stems and finish in another program.
