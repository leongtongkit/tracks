Free online music maker for students and Chromebooks

By Tongkit Leong · June 22, 2026 · 7 min read

The best free music maker for a Chromebook is one that runs entirely in the browser: open tracks.jfound.net in any tab, and you have a full multi-track studio with synths, drums, a mic recorder, and MP3/WAV export — no install, no account, and no cost, even on a school-managed device.

Why Chromebooks and students need a browser-based music tool

Chromebooks run ChromeOS, which does not support traditional software installs. You cannot run Ableton, GarageBand, FL Studio, or most desktop DAWs on a Chromebook at all. Even on Windows and Mac school devices, IT policies often block the installation of unauthorized apps.

A browser-based studio sidesteps all of that. Tracks is a website — the same kind of thing as Google Docs or YouTube. If the school's network allows students to access it, it works. No installer, no administrator password, no app permissions to request.

That also means it works on any device where a modern browser runs: a school Chromebook, a library computer, a home Windows laptop, or a Mac. Your project is saved in the browser's local storage, so it waits for you on whatever device you used last.

What Tracks can do

Tracks is not a toy. The feature set covers what most students and hobbyist producers need for a complete song:

  • Multi-track timeline — arrange clips across as many tracks as your project needs, with a piano roll for notes and a timeline for ordering sections.
  • Built-in instruments — a subtractive synthesizer, an FM synthesizer, 808 and 909 drum machines, a 16-pad sampler, and SoundFont (.sf2) playback for realistic piano, strings, brass, and other sounds.
  • Mic recording — record your voice or any acoustic sound through the device's built-in mic or a USB mic, with a count-in so you start on time.
  • Mixer with effects — per-track parametric EQ, compressor, gate, and reverb/delay sends. These are the same effect types used in professional studios, not simplified toy versions.
  • Autotune — snap vocal recordings to the nearest note in the key you set, with adjustable speed so it sounds subtle or robotic depending on what you want.
  • Stem extraction — upload a finished song and Tracks separates it into drums, bass, vocals, and other stems. Useful for remixing or studying how a track is put together.
  • Export — bounce the whole mix to a WAV or MP3 file that you can share, submit as an assignment, or upload anywhere.

Everything runs locally. Your projects never leave your device — there is no cloud account storing your work, and no subscription required to keep using any of these features. See What is a DAW? if you want more background on what these tools do and why they matter.

Getting started: your first session on a Chromebook

Open Chrome on your Chromebook and navigate to tracks.jfound.net/studio. The studio loads directly in the tab — nothing to install, nothing to sign into.

The first time you use the mic recorder, Chrome will ask for microphone access. Click Allow. That permission stays saved for the site, so you will not see the prompt again on that device.

If you want a guided walkthrough of the full song-making process from scratch, the beginner's guide to making a song covers every step: setting a tempo, adding tracks, writing a melody in the piano roll, programming drums, and exporting the result.

Making beats and melodies

The fastest way to start is with the drum machine. Add a drum track, open the step sequencer, and click the steps you want active. Each row is a different drum sound — kick, snare, hi-hat, and so on. The pattern loops automatically so you can hear changes in real time as you build it up.

For melody and chords, add an instrument track and open the piano roll. Draw notes by clicking in the grid. The horizontal axis is time, the vertical axis is pitch. You do not need to know music theory to experiment — move notes up and down until they sound right to you, and use the quantize button to tighten up the timing. There is a full guide to the drum machine at free online drum machine: program beats with no software if you want more detail on step sequencing.

The SoundFont player is particularly useful in a school music context. Load an .sf2 file and you get a full General MIDI instrument set — piano, guitar, bass, strings, brass, woodwinds — all playable from the piano roll or a connected MIDI keyboard. Many SoundFont files are free to download and use.

Recording your voice

To record a vocal part, add an audio track and click the mic icon to arm it for recording. Set your input level so the meter peaks in the green-to-yellow range without clipping into red. Press the main Record button in the transport and sing or speak through your mic.

After recording, Tracks' built-in autotune can correct pitch. Open the effect chain on the vocal track, enable autotune, and set the key and scale of your song. The Speed parameter controls how aggressively it corrects: a slow speed smooths pitch gradually and sounds natural; a fast speed snaps instantly and gives the robotic sound associated with certain pop and rap production styles.

If your Chromebook's built-in mic picks up too much room noise, a USB microphone will give cleaner results. Chrome on ChromeOS recognizes USB audio devices as soon as you plug them in — no driver needed.

Mixing your track

Mixing is the step where you balance all the parts so they sit together clearly. In Tracks, click the Mixer button to open the channel strip view. From here you can:

  • Adjust the volume fader on each track so no single instrument drowns out the others.
  • Pan tracks left or right to give the mix width — kick and bass usually stay in the center, while guitars, synths, and backing vocals can spread out.
  • Open the EQ on each track to cut frequencies that clash. For example, if your kick and bass both sound muddy together, a low-cut on the bass around 60–80 Hz can clean up the overlap.
  • Add reverb from the send/return section to give instruments a sense of shared space without washing out the entire mix.

You do not need to get the mix perfect on the first try. A few basic volume and pan adjustments go a long way. For more detail on EQ and compression, see How to mix a track: EQ, compression, and reverb basics.

Exporting and submitting your song

When your track is ready, go to File > Export in the Tracks menu. Choose MP3 for a smaller file that works everywhere, or WAV for the highest quality. The file downloads straight to your Chromebook's Downloads folder.

From there, you can submit it to Google Classroom as an attachment, upload it to SoundCloud, share it via Google Drive, or send it however the assignment requires. The exported file is a standard audio file — it plays in any media player or streaming app.

MP3 is the right choice for sharing or submitting. WAV is better if you plan to keep editing the file or hand it to someone else for mastering. There is a detailed breakdown of the difference at How to export your song to MP3 or WAV.

Frequently asked questions

Does Tracks work on a school-managed Chromebook?

In most cases, yes. Tracks is a website accessed through Chrome, not an app that requires installation. As long as the school's network or content filter does not block tracks.jfound.net, it works the same as any other website. If you are having trouble accessing it on school Wi-Fi, ask your teacher or IT department to whitelist the domain.

Do I need to create an account?

No. Tracks has no accounts. Open the studio and start making music. Your projects are saved in the browser's local storage automatically — nothing is uploaded to a server, and you do not need to log in to access your work.

Will my project be lost if I close the browser tab?

Your project is saved in your browser's local storage and will still be there when you reopen the tab on the same device in the same browser. To be safe — especially on a shared or school device where someone might clear browser data — export your project as a .tracks.json file from the File menu. You can reimport it later on any device running Tracks.

Can I use Tracks on a shared school Chromebook?

Yes, but be aware that local storage is tied to the browser profile. On a shared device, if you sign in to Chrome with your school Google account, your local storage will be separate from other students'. If you use the device without signing in, your data is stored in the guest or default profile, which may be cleared when you log out. Exporting your .tracks.json project file to your Google Drive or downloading it to a USB drive is the safest way to preserve work across sessions on shared hardware.

What is the difference between Tracks and BandLab or Soundtrap?

BandLab and Soundtrap save projects to their own cloud servers, which requires creating an account and trusting a third-party service with your recordings. Tracks saves everything locally on your device — nothing is uploaded. Tracks also runs without an account entirely, which is simpler for students who may not be able to create external accounts. The trade-off is that you are responsible for backing up your project files yourself.

Does Tracks work offline?

Once the studio is loaded in a browser tab, most of the core tools work without a network connection, since the audio engine runs entirely in the browser. However, for the most reliable experience — especially on first load — an internet connection is recommended so all assets download correctly.

Can my music teacher see or access my projects?

No. Because Tracks stores projects in your browser's local storage and never uploads them anywhere, no one else can access your projects through Tracks — not your teacher, not the developers. The only way to share your work is to export a file and send it yourself.
