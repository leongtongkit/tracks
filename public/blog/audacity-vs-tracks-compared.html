Audacity vs Tracks: free audio editor or free browser DAW?

By Tongkit Leong · July 20, 2026 · 8 min read

Audacity is a free, open-source audio editor for recording, cutting, and cleaning up sound files. Tracks is a free browser-based DAW for writing music from scratch — MIDI, synths, drum machines, and a full mixer. They solve different problems: reach for Audacity when you already have audio to fix, and reach for Tracks when you are building a song from nothing.

The core difference in one line

Audacity edits audio. Tracks composes music. Audacity opens an existing waveform — a podcast recording, a voice memo, a field recording — and lets you cut, amplify, filter, and export it. Tracks starts from an empty timeline and gives you the instruments, a piano roll, and a MIDI-and-audio mixer to write, arrange, and mix an original track. A lot of people land on one when they actually need the other, so it's worth being precise about what each tool is built for before you commit an afternoon to the wrong one.

Audacity

Audacity has been the default free audio editor for two decades. It is a desktop application (Windows, Mac, Linux) rather than a browser tool, and it is open source, which means no account, no subscription, and a large community of plugins built around it.

Where Audacity stands out

Audacity is excellent at exactly what it's designed for: recording a single source, then editing and cleaning that recording. Trimming silence, splicing together the best parts of a podcast, removing background hum with its noise-reduction tool, normalizing loudness, or converting a batch of files between formats — Audacity does all of this reliably and for free. Its spectral view is genuinely useful for finding and surgically removing a specific unwanted sound, like a click or a cough. Because it's a native desktop app, it also handles very long recordings (a two-hour interview, a full album side) without the memory constraints a browser tab can run into.

Where Audacity has limits

Audacity has no piano roll and no built-in synthesizers or drum machines. It supports basic tone generation and can host third-party VST/AU instrument plugins on some platforms, but composing a MIDI arrangement — writing a bassline, a chord progression, or a drum pattern note by note — is not what it's built for and the workflow shows it. There's no session view, no automation lanes in the modern DAW sense, and mixing tools are limited to basic effects rather than a per-track EQ/compressor/gate/send chain. It also requires a download and install, and as a native app, sharing a project means sharing files rather than opening a link.

Tracks

Tracks runs entirely in the browser, with no install and no account, and stores everything locally on your device — nothing is uploaded anywhere.

Writing music, not just editing it

Tracks gives you a multi-track timeline with a full piano roll — velocity, quantize, swing, a tempo map — plus a subtractive synth, an FM synth, 808/909 drum machines, a 16-pad sampler, and SoundFont (.sf2) playback. You can write a bassline, program a beat, and sketch chords without ever touching an external audio file. See what is a DAW for the general shape of this workflow if it's new to you.

Recording and editing audio too

Tracks isn't only a MIDI tool — it also records mic input directly in the browser, with count-in and take comping across multiple passes. Once recorded, a vocal or instrument take sits on the timeline as an audio clip you can trim, time-stretch, repitch, and process with the same mixer as everything else. It doesn't have Audacity's dedicated spectral-repair view for surgical noise removal, but for the common case — recording a part and getting it into a mix — the workflow is built in rather than a separate step.

Mixing and export

Every track gets a parametric EQ, a compressor, a gate, and a de-esser, plus reverb/delay sends, group buses, sidechain ducking, and automation on any parameter. See how to mix a track for the fundamentals. Export covers WAV and MP3 bounces, per-track stems, a standard MIDI file, and a self-contained project file you can reopen later or hand to someone else.

Which one should you use?

Choose Audacity if

You already have a recording — a podcast, an interview, a field recording, a vocal memo — and you need to trim it, remove noise, normalize it, or convert its format. Audacity's spectral editing and noise-reduction tools are more specialized than anything in a general-purpose DAW, and for long-form spoken-word audio it's the more efficient tool.

Choose Tracks if

You're starting from nothing and want to write an actual song — a beat, a bassline, a melody, a full arrangement — with real instruments and a proper mixer, without installing anything. Tracks is also the better choice if you're recording a vocal or instrument part that needs to sit inside a multi-track mix rather than stand alone, since the recording, the mixing, and the instruments all live in the same project.

Use both

These tools aren't mutually exclusive. Some producers record and clean up a raw vocal or sample in Audacity, then drag the finished file into a Tracks project to arrange and mix it against synths and drums. If you already have Audacity in your workflow for editing, there's no reason to give it up — just don't expect it to replace a DAW's instruments and mixer, and don't expect a DAW to replace Audacity's dedicated repair tools.

Frequently asked questions

Can Audacity do MIDI and synths?

Not natively. Audacity has a basic tone generator and limited support for hosting instrument plugins on some platforms, but it has no piano roll and isn't built for composing a MIDI arrangement. For writing music with synths, drum machines, or MIDI, a DAW like Tracks is the more direct tool.

Is Tracks a replacement for Audacity's noise reduction?

Partially. Tracks includes a noise gate, EQ, and a de-esser, which handle the most common cleanup jobs — room noise between phrases, harsh sibilance, tonal balance. It doesn't have Audacity's spectral-repair view for surgically removing a specific noise (like a click or a cough) from inside a waveform. If that's your main task, Audacity is still the sharper tool.

Does Tracks require a download like Audacity does?

No. Tracks runs entirely in the browser at tracks.jfound.net/studio — no install, no account. Audacity is a native desktop application you download and install once, after which it also works offline.

Is my audio uploaded anywhere in either tool?

No, in both cases. Audacity is a local desktop app by nature. Tracks runs in the browser but is also local-only — your project and any audio you record or import stay on your device and are never uploaded to a server.

Which one is better for a podcast?

Audacity. Long-form spoken-word editing — trimming, noise reduction, loudness normalization across a long file — is exactly what it was built for. Tracks is built for music production and doesn't have Audacity's podcast-specific editing tools.

Which one is better for making a beat or a song?

Tracks. It has the instruments, piano roll, and mixer that music production actually needs. See how to make a song in your browser for a step-by-step starting point.
