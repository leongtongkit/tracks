Best free VST alternative instruments in the browser

By Tongkit Leong · July 1, 2026 · 8 min read

You don't need paid VST plugins to write a full track. Tracks, a free browser studio, ships a subtractive synth, an FM synth, 808 and 909 drum machines, a 16-pad sampler, and SoundFont (.sf2) playback built in — no plugin installs, no licenses, no downloads.

Why "free VST alternative" usually means a letdown

Most free instrument plugins are a trap: a stripped-down demo that nags you to upgrade, a shaky third-party VST that only half-installs on your system, or a huge download that eats a gigabyte for one bass patch. And before you get to any of that, you need a host DAW installed to load the plugin into in the first place. A browser studio skips all of it — the instruments are already loaded in the page, ready to play the moment you open a track.

The instruments built into Tracks

Subtractive synth

A classic analog-style synth: oscillators, a filter, and an amp envelope. This is your workhorse for basses, pads, leads, and stabs — the same signal chain that powers most hardware and software synths, tuned for fast sound design rather than a wall of menus. Start from a preset, then move the filter cutoff and envelope until it sits where you want in the mix.

FM synth

Frequency modulation synthesis gets you sounds a subtractive synth can't easily reach — glassy bells, metallic keys, aggressive basses, and the kind of clean electric-piano tones associated with classic FM hardware. FM is less forgiving to program by ear than a filter synth, so it's worth starting from a preset and nudging one operator at a time to hear what each one contributes.

808 and 909 drum machines

Two drum machine emulations cover most modern genres between them. The 808-style kit gives you the deep, tunable sub kick, snappy claps, and open/closed hi-hats behind trap, hip-hop, and R&B. The 909-style kit gives you the punchier, more clipped kick and snare that drive house and techno. Both are step-programmable with swing and per-hit velocity, so a beat doesn't have to sound like it was typed into a grid.

16-pad sampler

Drop your own one-shots, vocal chops, or loops onto 16 pads and trigger them from the piano roll or a MIDI controller. This is the fastest route to a genuinely original sound — record a sample yourself, chop it, and it's now an instrument nobody else has.

SoundFont (.sf2) playback

For orchestral, band, and general-purpose sounds — piano, strings, brass, guitar, and more — Tracks can load and play standard SoundFont (.sf2) banks. SoundFonts are a long-established free format with thousands of banks available online, so you're not limited to what ships in the studio if you want a specific instrument sound.

Putting instruments together in a track

  1. Start with drums. Program a beat on the 808 or 909 machine to set the tempo and feel before you write anything melodic.
  2. Add a bass. The subtractive synth's low end will sit best under the kick — check sidechain compression if the two are fighting for space.
  3. Layer a lead or pad. Reach for the FM synth when you want a brighter or more textured tone than the subtractive synth gives you.
  4. Drop in a sample or SoundFont part for anything the synths can't cover — a real piano, a vocal chop, a horn stab.
  5. Mix it. EQ, compression, and reverb tie the instruments into one track — see the mixing basics guide for the fundamentals.

What a browser studio can't do (yet)

Being straightforward about the limits: Tracks doesn't support loading third-party VST or AU plugins the way a desktop DAW does, so if your workflow depends on one specific paid plugin, you'll still need a native host for that. What it does have is a self-contained set of instruments capable of finishing a real track — synths, drums, sampling, and SoundFont playback — without ever touching an installer.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to install anything to use these instruments?

No. Every instrument in Tracks runs in the browser tab itself using the Web Audio API — there's nothing to download or license.

Can I use my own samples?

Yes. The 16-pad sampler accepts your own audio files, and you can also load standard SoundFont (.sf2) banks for orchestral and acoustic sounds.

Are my instrument sounds or projects uploaded anywhere?

No. Tracks runs entirely on your device — your projects and any samples you load never leave your browser.

Is this good enough to replace a paid VST library?

For most songwriting, beat-making, and demo work, yes — the synths, drum machines, sampler, and SoundFont playback cover the core instrument needs of a full track. If you rely on one specific specialist plugin, you may still want it alongside a browser studio rather than instead of one.
