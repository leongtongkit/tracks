BandLab vs Soundtrap vs Tracks: free browser studios compared

By Tongkit Leong · June 27, 2026 · 8 min read

BandLab, Soundtrap, and Tracks are the three free browser DAWs most people encounter — BandLab is built around social sharing and cloud collaboration, Soundtrap targets real-time co-production and education, and Tracks runs entirely on your device with no account, no upload, and the deepest mixing toolset of the three.

What all three have in common

Before getting into the differences, it helps to mark the common ground. All three tools:

  • Run in a desktop web browser with no software download required.
  • Offer multi-track recording, a piano roll or step sequencer, and the ability to layer instruments and audio clips.
  • Let you export a finished mix — at minimum as a stereo audio file — at no cost.
  • Support mic recording through the browser's audio API.

That shared foundation is worth naming, because it means the differences are about workflow, philosophy, and feature depth rather than fundamental capability. You can finish a song in any of them. The question is which one removes the most friction for the way you specifically want to work.

BandLab

BandLab is a social music platform first and a DAW second. The browser studio is competent — it includes a multi-track timeline, a loop library, virtual guitar amps and effects, basic MIDI instruments, and a feed of public projects you can remix or collaborate on. It is free, and unlike Soundtrap it does not gate core functionality behind a paid plan.

Where BandLab stands out

If you want to share your work publicly or collaborate asynchronously — someone adds a guitar part, someone else adds vocals, a third person mixes — BandLab's social layer is the most developed of the three. You get a profile, followers, a discovery feed, and the ability to remix any public track with a single click. For a producer who wants an audience as part of the creative process, that integration is genuinely useful. BandLab also provides unlimited cloud storage for project files on the free plan, which is notable.

Where BandLab has limits

Because BandLab is cloud-native, your projects live on BandLab's servers. An account is required, and removing your account means losing your project history. The virtual instruments lean toward preset-heavy, consumer-oriented tones rather than deep synthesis controls — if you want to build your own synth patch from oscillators, you will feel constrained. The mixer is functional but does not offer the kind of parametric EQ, sidechain routing, or bus architecture that a mixing-focused workflow needs. The interface is optimized for quick loop-based tracks rather than complex arrangements.

Soundtrap

Soundtrap (owned by Spotify) positions itself primarily as a tool for education and real-time collaboration. The browser DAW is polished and accessible — it offers a clean timeline, a solid loop library, basic virtual instruments, and the ability to invite collaborators to work on a session simultaneously in the same browser window.

Where Soundtrap stands out

Real-time collaborative editing is Soundtrap's strongest differentiator. Two or more people can be in the same project at the same time, hear each other's changes live, and communicate via the built-in chat panel. For a classroom setting, a remote writing session with a co-producer, or a band that wants to sketch ideas together across distance, that real-time model is the most seamless available in any free browser tool. Soundtrap for Education extends this with teacher dashboards and assignment tools. The interface is intentionally simple, which makes it a low-friction first DAW for beginners.

Where Soundtrap has limits

The free tier is restricted. The number of projects, the loop library access, and some export options are capped unless you pay for a Soundtrap Studio subscription. If you hit those limits, you either pay or export what you have and start over. Like BandLab, Soundtrap requires an account and stores all projects in the cloud. The synthesis and mixing depth is limited — there is no parametric EQ, no sidechain compression, no automation lanes for individual parameters, and no way to load your own SoundFont or sampler content in the free tier. As a result, Soundtrap tends to work well for sketching and collaboration but becomes a constraint when you try to push a mix to a finished, well-balanced state.

Tracks

Tracks takes a different approach from both: it is a local-only studio that runs in the browser but stores nothing outside your device. No account is required, no project data is ever uploaded, and there are no tier limits — every feature is available from the moment you open the tab.

Instruments and sound design

Tracks includes two synthesis engines — a subtractive synth and an FM synth — alongside an 808/909-style drum machine, a 16-pad sampler, and SoundFont (.sf2) playback. You can build sounds from scratch rather than selecting from a fixed preset list. The drum machine supports individual sample import if you want to replace the default kits with your own audio files. For producers who want to design their own sounds rather than browse a loop library, this is a material difference.

Recording and MIDI

Tracks supports mic recording with count-in and take comping — you record multiple passes and comp the best moments into a single performance — and MIDI recording via Web MIDI if you have a keyboard or pad controller connected over USB. See how to use a MIDI keyboard in a browser DAW for the setup steps. The piano roll includes velocity editing, quantize, swing, and a tempo map.

Mixing

The mixer in Tracks is the deepest of the three tools compared here. Each track has a parametric EQ, a compressor, a gate, and a de-esser. There are reverb and delay send buses, group buses, sidechain ducking between tracks, and automation of any parameter across the timeline. Vocal processing includes autotune with pitch correction and time-stretch/repitch for adjusting timing without changing pitch. The mixer tools described in how to mix a track: EQ, compression, and reverb basics are all present and available on the free tier — which is the only tier.

Export

You can export a finished mix as WAV or MP3, export individual track stems as separate WAV files, export a standard MIDI file, and save the full project as a portable .tracks.json file. Nothing requires a paid plan. Your projects are stored in your browser's local storage and can be exported as a project file and reopened later — but because storage is local, clearing your browser's site data would remove them, so export the project file if you want a permanent backup outside the browser.

Privacy and data

Because Tracks is local-only, there is no account to create, no password to manage, and no data to expose. Your audio files and project data never leave your device. For producers working with uncleared samples, vocal recordings of other people, or simply personal material they do not want on a third-party server, that architecture is the practical default rather than a premium feature.

Which one should you use?

Choose BandLab if

You want to share your music publicly, build an audience, or collaborate asynchronously with producers and musicians you have met online. BandLab's social layer and unlimited cloud storage make it the best free option for a producer who treats the platform as part of the creative process — discovering collaborators, getting feedback, and remixing other people's work.

Choose Soundtrap if

You are working in an education setting — classroom, remote lesson, or co-writing session — where real-time collaboration in the same browser session is the primary need. Soundtrap's live co-editing is the most polished of any free browser tool, and its Soundtrap for Education product integrates with school accounts and assignment workflows. Just be aware of the free tier limits if you are using it for personal projects rather than a structured class.

Choose Tracks if

You want to make a fully produced, mixed track with deep synthesis, sidechain compression, parametric EQ on every channel, and stem export — without creating an account or sending your audio to any server. Tracks is the right choice when production depth and privacy matter more than social features. It is also the only one of the three that gives you stem extraction from imported audio, which makes it useful for remixing, sampling, and practice.

Frequently asked questions

Is BandLab actually free?

Yes — BandLab's core studio and cloud storage are free with a registered account. The platform occasionally offers premium content packs (additional instrument sounds, loops) for purchase, but the DAW itself, the collaboration tools, and the social features do not require a paid plan. This distinguishes it from Soundtrap, where some features are locked behind a paid tier.

Is Soundtrap free?

Soundtrap has a free tier, but it limits the number of projects you can save, the loop library you can access, and some export functionality. If you are using it for light personal use or in a school that has a site license, those limits may not affect you. For sustained personal production, most users hit the free tier ceiling and either pay or look for an alternative.

Do any of these work offline?

BandLab and Soundtrap require an internet connection because they are cloud-based — the projects live on their servers. Tracks is local-only by design, but it still requires a browser tab and a network connection for the initial page load. Once the page has loaded, the audio engine runs entirely in the browser; you could theoretically finish a session after losing the connection, but you would not be able to refresh the tab. For offline production, a desktop DAW is the more reliable choice.

Which free browser DAW is best for beginners?

Soundtrap is the most intentionally beginner-friendly interface of the three — clean, minimal, and guided. BandLab is also accessible and gives beginners a social context for their work. Tracks has more features overall, which means more to learn, but the guide to making your first song in the browser covers the core workflow step by step and the studio is usable without creating an account or signing up for anything.

Can I use a MIDI keyboard with any of these?

Tracks supports Web MIDI natively — connect a USB MIDI keyboard, open the browser, and it appears as an input device with no drivers or configuration. BandLab and Soundtrap also have some MIDI input support, but the implementation depth varies by browser and device. For the most complete MIDI recording workflow in a browser, Tracks is the most consistently reliable of the three.

Does Tracks have any collaboration features?

Not in the sense that BandLab and Soundtrap do. Because Tracks is local-only, there is no built-in mechanism for two people to work on the same project remotely. You can export the .tracks.json project file, share it with a collaborator, and have them open it in their own browser — but that is asynchronous file exchange rather than a connected session. Real-time co-editing is not a feature of Tracks.
